How APEX Works

APEX is a two-stage residual model. Stage one is the draft market itself - where a player was picked. Stage two is what the model knows that the market might not. APEX+ is the calibrated combination of the two, and it beats both on out-of-time data.

Validation update: the APEX+ 3.5× amplification described in sections 4-5 did not survive regenerated public-source rolling validation (2011-2021 year-by-year backtests). The public board now uses profile-only raw APEX blended conservatively with the market (Conservative 0.50), worth about +0.012 mean Spearman over draft slot with an 8/11 yearly win rate. Amplification and NCAA production features remain experimental. Pre-draft, the model matches ESPN consensus but does not beat it. See the current validation status for the numbers that hold up.

01What the model predicts

The target is within-class Career AV percentile: take every player in a draft class, rank their career Approximate Value (Pro-Football-Reference's all-position value stat), and convert to a 0–100 percentile. A 90 means a top-10% career for that class; a 25 means a bottom-quartile one.

Ranking within class, rather than using raw AV, neutralizes era effects - longer careers in older data, scoring inflation, rule changes - so a 2003 grade and a 2023 grade mean the same thing.

02Stage one: the pick market

Draft position is the single strongest public predictor of career outcome - 32 front offices pricing a player is hard to beat. Stage one fits the historical curve of expected career percentile by pick number (the dashed amber curve on the board's pick-to-outcome map). Pick #1 has produced roughly a 98th-percentile career on average; pick #100 about a 64th; pick #250 about a 45th.

This curve alone reaches a Spearman rank correlation of ρ = 0.615 with actual outcomes on the holdout. Every claim the model makes is measured against this baseline - if APEX can't beat the market, it isn't adding anything.

03Stage two: the model's read

Stage two models the residual: how much a player should outperform or underperform their draft slot, using college production, athletic profile, position, school context, and draft-capital interactions. The output is the raw APEX grade, and the difference between it and the market curve is the edge:

edge = APEX_raw − market(pick)

The edge is where the model earns its keep. Among 2000–2019 draftees, players the model graded +2 points or more above their slot went on to beat the market 84.6% of the time (mean outperformance +12.8 percentile points). Players graded −2 or worse beat the market only 17.2% of the time (mean −19.5 points). A neutral read was a coin flip, as it should be.

04APEX+: amplifying the disagreement

v1.2's headline change. Backtesting revealed that the raw model systematically under-trusts its own residual: its disagreements with the market pointed the right direction but were too small in magnitude. Scaling the residual recovers that lost signal:

APEX+ = clip( market(pick) + 3.5 × (APEX_raw − market(pick)) , 1, 99 )

The amplification factor was fit out-of-time and is remarkably stable - the optimum lands between ×3 and ×4 in every multi-year window from 2008 to 2020:

Intuition: when a tree-based residual model is regularized to avoid overfitting, its predictions get shrunk toward zero. The direction survives; the magnitude doesn't. The ×3.5 rescale undoes that shrinkage where it's been verified to help. It changes no rankings driven purely by draft slot - it only widens the gap when the model and market disagree.

05Calibration: turning a grade into odds

A "73rd-percentile projection" is abstract. Calibration makes it concrete: among the 4,396 drafted players from 2000–2019, how did players at each APEX+ level actually turn out? Outcomes are bucketed into four tiers by final career percentile: Star (≥85), Starter (70–85), Contributor (45–70), Minimal (<45).

This is also why the board shows probability bars instead of single labels. A 65 grade isn't "a Contributor" - it's a player who hits Starter-or-better half the time and busts one time in eight.

06Reading a player card

Every drafted player's card has four parts, in the order you should read them:

  • The projection ribbon. A 0–100 percentile track. The blue pin is the market expectation for the draft slot; the amber diamond is APEX+. The shaded span between them is the model's disagreement - green if APEX+ is higher, red if lower. For graded classes, a white pin marks the actual outcome, so you can see at a glance who was right.
  • Calibrated outcome odds. The stacked bar from the table above, for this player's exact grade band - real frequencies, not vibes.
  • Why this grade. The three-step arithmetic spelled out with this player's numbers: market baseline → model read → ×3.5 amplification.
  • Closest historical comps. The five settled-career players (2000–2019) at the same position group with the nearest APEX+ and market profile, with how each turned out. If 4 of 5 comps hit, that tells you more than any single number.

07Class coverage: 2024, 2025, 2026

2000–2023 - graded classes. Careers are settled (or nearly so for 2020–2023); the "Actual" column is meaningful, and 2000–2019 players power calibration and comps.

2024 - early returns. These players were drafted, so they get full APEX+ projections, ribbons, odds, and comps. But the "Actual" column reflects only one season of Career AV and is flagged provisional on the card: one-year percentiles overweight immediate playing time, and a redshirted rookie can still hit. Trust the projection over the early actual.

2025 & 2026 - drafted, projections live. Actual draft results (every pick, rounds 1–7) are now merged into the dataset for both classes. Each drafted player gets a market baseline from their real slot (position-aware historical lookup), the model's pre-draft residual read as the edge, and a full APEX+ projection with calibrated odds, ribbon, and comps. The DB gate is preserved: the model declines to disagree with the market at DB, exactly as it does historically. NFL outcomes for these classes are not yet folded in, so cards are pure projection - flagged "rookie" (2026) or "yr 1" (2025). Watchlist players who went unselected appear as UDFA with their raw read only, since APEX+ tiers are anchored to draft capital. Late-round picks the model never graded pre-draft (mostly small-school Day 3 selections) are not shown.

08Validation & honest limitations

  • Out-of-time holdout. The headline ρ = 0.697 is on 2012–2014, never touched in training, and the amplification factor reproduces across four independent windows. That said, historical APEX grades shown for the deepest training years may be partially in-sample - treat the very early classes' "edge" as descriptive, not predictive proof.
  • Career AV is imperfect. It undervalues specialists and run-blocking, overvalues volume, and compresses differences at the top. It's the best public all-position outcome stat, not the truth.
  • Positions differ. The model's residual adds the most signal at RB, QB, and TE, solid signal at WR, DT, and LB, and essentially nothing at DB in some eras (the board shows zero edges there - the model declines to disagree rather than inventing one).
  • Comps are profile matches, not scouting matches. Two players with identical APEX+ and draft capital can be wildly different athletes. Comps tell you how this statistical profile resolves, nothing more.
  • Probabilities are base rates. The odds bars are historical frequencies for a grade band. They don't know about injuries, scheme fit, or character - and neither does the model.

09Glossary & changelog

  • Career AV percentile (Actual) - within-class rank of career Approximate Value, 0–100.
  • Market - expected career percentile for a draft slot, fit on 2000–2019 history.
  • Raw APEX - market plus the stage-two model's residual read.
  • Edge - raw APEX minus market. The model's disagreement with the league.
  • APEX+ - market + 3.5 × edge, clipped to 1–99. The board's headline projection.
  • Tiers - Star ≥85 · Starter 70–85 · Contributor 45–70 · Minimal <45.
